Chapter VI

The Gambler's Fallacy

The most dangerous cognitive bias in gambling — the false belief that past outcomes influence future independent events. Debunked here with mathematical precision.

What is the Gambler's Fallacy?

The Gambler's Fallacy (also known as the Monte Carlo Fallacy or the Fallacy of the Maturity of Chances) is the erroneous belief that if a random event has occurred more frequently than expected in the past, it is less likely to occur in the future — or vice versa.

In roulette: after seeing 10 consecutive red results, many players believe "black is due." This intuition is mathematically false.

P(black | last 10 were red) = P(black) = 18/37
≈ 48.6486%

The wheel has no memory.

Why the Intuition Fails

The fallacy conflates two different probability statements:

TRUE (before any spins)
P(10 reds in a row) = (18/37)¹⁰ ≈ 0.000649
Very unlikely — for a fresh sequence.
FALSE belief (after 9 reds have already occurred)
P(10th red | 9 reds already) ≠ very small
= P(red) = 18/37 ≈ 48.65%

Once 9 reds have occurred, they are fixed history. The conditional probability given them equals the unconditional probability. This is the formal definition of statistical independence.

Formal Proof: Independence of Roulette Spins

Two events A and B are statistically independent if and only if:

P(A ∩ B) = P(A) · P(B)  ↔  P(A | B) = P(A)

For roulette spins S₁, S₂, S₃, … each spin is drawn from the same uniform distribution over {0, 1, …, 36} with replacement. The wheel and ball are reset identically each spin:

P(Sₙ₊₁ = red | S₁ = r₁, …, Sₙ = rₙ)
= P(Sₙ₊₁ = red)
= 18/37 ≈ 0.48649…

∀ r₁, …, rₙ ∈ {0,…,36}, ∀ n ∈ ℕ

This follows directly from the memoryless property of i.i.d. random variables. The physical system has no state memory between spins. Past outcomes carry zero information about future outcomes.

Historical Context

The Monte Carlo Incident, 1913

On August 18, 1913, at the Casino de Monte-Carlo, the roulette ball fell on black an extraordinary 26 consecutive times. As the streak continued, gamblers lost millions of francs betting on red, convinced that "red must be due."

This event — which gave the Gambler's Fallacy its alternative name, the Monte Carlo Fallacy — is the most famous real-world demonstration of the bias.

P(26 consecutive blacks from scratch) = (18/37)²⁶ ≈ 0.00000033 (1 in 3,000,000)
P(black on 27th spin | 26 already occurred) = 18/37 ≈ 48.65%

The streak was extraordinary. The 27th spin outcome was entirely ordinary.

With millions of roulette spins globally per year, even one-in-a-million events are routine. Remarkable streaks do not signal impending corrections — they are simply the inevitable tail events of any sufficiently long sequence of independent trials. Once they have occurred, they carry no information about what comes next.
